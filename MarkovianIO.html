A review of the Input-Output model

A review of the Input-Output model

Ivan Pacheco Soto1
Tec de Monterrey

July 2008

Abstract

We review the mathematical formulation of the basic input-output model in Economy. We present some necessary and sufficient conditions for this model to hold for non-negative components. In particular, we emphasize the use of a Markovian approach developed by Kemeny and Snell [1976], which provides the weakest conditions for the input-output model to hold, and it provides a very intuitive interpretation of the flows in this model. At the end, we apply this approach to analyze the transaction matrix of the national economy of Mexico for the year 2003.

Preface

The original idea for this work was to find a statistical technique to identify clusters of industries, i.e., a quantitative method to identify the economic and geographic concentration of companies and institutions in a particular field. Some methods as the location quotient, the spatial Hirschman-Herfindaht index and the locational Gini coefficient were considered initially as numerical measures to quantify this agglomeration of industries. Unfortunately these techniques considered one industry at a time, and they do not identify the interrelation among the industries in order to be able to cluster industries.

Feser and Bergman [2000] use the input-output matrix to identify 23 industry clusters in the U.S. economy, and then use these clusters as a template for a regional cluster analysis. We are interested in following this work to identify similar cluster in the mexican economy and apply this template for regional study in the State of Zacatecas. A very similar approach was taken by Sánchez Gamboa and Bracamonte Sierra [2006] in the analysis of the automotive cluster in the city of Hermosillo, Mexico. In this approach, a linkage matrix, made of the correlation among the columns or the rows of the input-output matrix, is constructed and a principal component analysis with varimax rotation is applied to this matrix. Then the components from this analysis are interpreted to identify the industry clusters.

The idea of applying the multivariate technique of factorial analysis to the linkage matrix come basically from the work of Czamanski [1974], and some critics on the interpretation of the results obtained can be found in Howe [1991]. Although the interpretation of the results is fundamental to any statistical analysis, it is also important to understand the type of data and the method of analysis. A principal component or a factorial analysis requires more cases than variables to obtain consistent results, and the linkage matrix is a square matrix, so when the covariance or correlation matrix is used to construct the components, it turns out that it is singular. Hence only the component from the principal component analysis can be used, for example, any criterion on maximum likelihood is inconsistent. Furthermore these multivariate techniques are applied to general quantitative variables, without the restrictions that we can find in a input-output matrix or in the linkage matrix. Hence we felt that a deeper understanding of the mathematical input-output model was required, and the review of this material is presented in this work. We expect, in the future, this kind of information can be included in the framework of a principal component analysis, which would be an improvement on the methodology suggested by Feser and Bergman [2000].

1  Introduction

The input-output analysis is a theoretical framework to quantify the mutual interrelations among industries in a nation's economy. These relations can be represented by tables or matrices, as shown below. The first input-output model was formulated in Leontief [1936], where Leontief constructed the corresponding table for the economy of the United States for the year 1919, and later on the one for the year 1929. In his work he visualized the economy of a whole country as if it were covered by one huge accounting system. This representation helps to predict and analyze effects of changes in one industry on the other industries. Leontief was awarded a Nobel prize in Economics in 1973 for the development of this methodology.

2  Leontief model

Leontief [1986] presents the interrelations among industries with respect to intermediate flows, final demand deliveries, and value added inputs. In this open system the final demand is considered as exogenous, so there are factor endowments, like labor, that have to be paid. This is reflected in a production function. Leontief uses a special production function where the inputs used in producing a product are related by a linear and fixed coefficient production function, i.e.,

xij = aijXj     for i,j = 1,2,...,n.

The matrix A = (aij)n ×n is called the matrix of interindustry coefficients. According to Nations [1999], the assumption of linear and fixed coefficients can be justified ön the notion that a technique of production will not change much over a short period and, even if it does change, the engineering data or statistical data collected by censuses on inputs utilized by an industry, which represent a new technology in an input-output column, can be obtain to replace the old column". Rao and Rao [1998] comments that for this open model the problem is to determine a production schedule meeting the internal and external demands.

In this table, the entries of the jth column indicates the amount of input of each kind that is required to produce one unit of output of industry j. On the other hand, the entries of the ith row indicates the amount of output from industry i to produce one unit of output of each kind. When the final demand Y is included, we get the basic input-output model, that can be expressed in matrix form as

AX+Y = X        or        (I-A)X=Y.
(1)

For this equation to hold, it is required not only the inverse of (I-A) to exist, but also to be non-negative. Note that the total output X and final demand X are non-negative, so it is also logical to require the inverse of (I-A) to be non-negative. We will review some work of Rao and Rao [1998], Seneta [2006] and Kemeny and Snell [1976] in this direction.

Rao and Rao [1998] points out that if the matrix (I-A) is nonsingular and non-negative, then a non-negative solution to this system is given by

X = (I-A)-1Y,
(2)

where (I-A)-1 is called the Leontief inverse. It is also known as the multiplier matrix or matrix of multipliers. Furthermore, Rao and Rao [1998] gives the following result to determine a non-negative solution (non-negative total output) of the basic input-output model.

Lemma 1 Let A Î Mn be a non-negative matrix. Then (I-A)-1 exists and non-negative if and only if there exists a non-negative vector x such that x > Ax. (The condition x > Ax means that there is some production schedule x such that each industry produces more than it consumes.)

From this result, it follows a sufficient condition to obtain a non-negative Leontief inverse. First we note that the sum of the entries of the jth column of A, the coefficients of the interindustry matrix, indicates the total amount of input from each industry that is required to produce one unit of output of industry j. If this sum is equal to one, it would indicate that this industry is not profitable. Therefore, we could require all industries to be profitable, i.e.,

It seems that for the Leontief inverse to be non-negative is only necessary this inverse to exists. Actually if the industry is not only profitless, but also all the output of this industry is also all the required input (coefficient equal to 1 in the diagonal of A), i.e., it is not related to another industry at all and it consumes itself, then the Leontief inverse does not exists.

Kemeny and Snell [1976] solves this general situation by embedding the input-output model in a Markov chain. The authors use a finite Markov chain to model the flow from one industry to another and define an additional absorbing state s, that they called the banking state. Thus for each dollar that industry i produces as output, it spends aij dollars as input from industry j, j=1,...,n. Then the remaining amount is considered a profit (value added), and can be thought as being deposited in a bank. Once the money gets to the bank, it is not spent, so the bank becomes an absorbing state. Although this approach gives a better understanding on the interindustry relations, it seems it has not been very popular in field of Economics. We present the results obtained by Kemeny and Snell [1976] and extend this idea for the price model.

The process of producing an item generates a long chain of interaction in the production processes. To produce a particular item requires inputs from other industries, and these, in turn, will require more inputs, and so on. Therefore each cycle of input requirements requires again another cycle. Nations [1999] calls this process a chain reactions or chain of interactions, and it applies this argument to interpret the Leontief inverse, but we consider this interpretation better fitted for the Markov chain approach.

Lemma 2 The following algorithm for the classification of states identify industries that can support a demand.

  1. Make a check opposite each row of A¢ whose row-sum is less than 1; that is, check each row corresponding to a profitable industry.

  2. Check the columns having the same indices as the rows already marked and then check, in theses columns, rows which have positive entries.

  3. Iterate (b) until it produces no new rows. Then one of two possibilities may occur:

    1. All rows are checked.

    2. Not all rows are checked.

If case (3a) occurs then the associated Markov chain is absorbing with the single absorbing state 0. Hence any non-negative demand can be met. If case (3b) occurs, then the rows which are not checked correspond to the maximal profitless closed group. Then we can find all states depending on these by marking these groups (removing previous check marks) and applying (2) repeatedly. Any states so marked will not be able to fulfill an outside demand. The industries which can meet no outside demand form a totally useless segment of the economy, and can be eliminated from the analysis. Then (I-A¢)-1 exists.

Seneta [2006] uses a stronger condition on the matrix of interindustry coefficients A to analyze the input-output model. He assumed that the matrix A is irreducible, so a necessary and sufficient condition for the existence of a non-negative solution X to the input-output model (1) for any non-negative demand Y is that the Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue r of A is strictly less than 1 (See Seber [2008].). The Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue is r < 1 if the sum of the entries of the columns (or rows) of the matrix A do not exceed unity and at least one is less than unity. In terms of profitable industries we could say that r < 1 if at least one of the industries is profitable, so at least one industry is able to pay factor like labor and capital. Note that here we are assuming that the matrix A is irreducible. For example, under this assumption, an industry could not have a zero input from the other industries, that is the case for the input-output matrix from Mexico for the year 2006.
